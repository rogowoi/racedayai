You spent 16 weeks nailing your training, dialing in nutrition, and building a race plan around target watts and goal pace. Then race morning arrives and it's 34 degrees with 80% humidity. Or the forecast calls for 40km/h crosswinds and intermittent rain.

Your plan isn't wrong. It just needs adjusting.

This guide covers the specific adjustments to make for every weather scenario you'll face in a 70.3, with concrete numbers rather than "just slow down."

## Heat: The Most Common Race-Day Challenge

Heat is the weather variable that costs the most time and causes the most DNFs in IRONMAN 70.3 racing. Understanding the physiology tells you exactly why — and how much to adjust.

### What Heat Does to Your Body

When ambient temperature rises above 25 degrees Celsius, your cardiovascular system faces a competing demand: deliver oxygen to working muscles and divert blood to the skin for cooling. As core temperature climbs, your body increasingly prioritizes cooling over performance. The result is cardiac drift — your heart rate rises 10-20 beats per minute at the same power output — and your muscles receive less oxygenated blood per stroke.

At the same time, gut blood flow decreases by up to 20% in hot conditions, which means the carbohydrates you're consuming are absorbed more slowly. This is why athletes who race at goal pace in the heat often report GI distress: the gut literally can't process the fuel at the rate they're delivering it.

World Triathlon uses the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) scale to assess heat stress for race conditions. The thresholds are worth knowing:

- **Green flag (below 25.7 degrees WBGT):** Low heat stress. Race your plan as-is.
- **Blue flag (25.7-27.8 degrees WBGT):** Moderate heat stress. Minor adjustments needed.
- **Orange flag (27.9-30.0 degrees WBGT):** High heat stress. Meaningful pacing changes required.
- **Red flag (30.1-32.2 degrees WBGT):** Very high heat stress. Significant reductions across all disciplines.
- **Black flag (above 32.2 degrees WBGT):** Extreme. Race may be shortened or cancelled.

Research on triathletes racing in WBGT above 26 degrees found that core temperatures exceeded 40 degrees Celsius in over 25% of participants — even in sprint-distance events. In a 70.3 lasting 4.5-7 hours, the thermal load is substantially greater.

### Specific Heat Adjustments

**Bike power:** Reduce your target power by 3-5% for every 5 degrees Celsius above 20 degrees. If your normal target is 180W and race day is 32 degrees, your adjusted target is 166-172W. This isn't optional. The power you can sustain at a given metabolic cost drops in heat regardless of how tough you think you are.

**Run pace:** Expect a 2-4% pace reduction for every 5 degrees above 20 degrees Celsius. A 5:30/km target becomes 5:45-5:55/km at 30 degrees. For races above 32 degrees, plan for 6-8% slower than your cool-weather target. The marathon data from the Ironman World Championship in Kona consistently shows this magnitude of pace loss even among professionals.

**Nutrition:** Increase fluid intake to 800-1,000ml per hour on the bike (up from a typical 500-750ml). Add 200-300mg of sodium per hour above your baseline. If you normally consume 600mg sodium per hour, push to 800-900mg. Reduce the concentration of your drink mix if needed — a more dilute solution absorbs faster in heat.

**Cooling:** Use ice at every aid station. Stuff ice under your tri suit at the front and back of neck. Pour water over your head and forearms. Pre-cool before the race by wearing an ice vest during warm-up or drinking an ice slurry 30 minutes before the start. Research shows pre-cooling can lower starting core temperature by 0.3-0.5 degrees, which effectively buys you 15-20 minutes of additional racing before thermal limits start degrading performance.

### Heat Acclimatization

If you know your race will be hot, 7-10 days of heat exposure before the event makes a measurable difference. Training for 60-90 minutes per day at moderate intensity (60-70% of max heart rate) in hot conditions triggers adaptations: your plasma volume increases, your sweat rate rises, and your resting core temperature drops. Athletes who acclimate typically regain 50-75% of the performance they would otherwise lose to heat.

If you can't train in heat, hot baths or sauna sessions after training for 20-30 minutes over 5-7 days provide a partial substitute.

## Cold: The Underrated Performance Thief

Cold weather in triathlon is less discussed than heat but can be just as disruptive, particularly on the bike where wind chill compounds the problem.

### What Cold Does to Performance

Below 12 degrees Celsius, your body diverts blood from extremities to protect your core. Muscle contractile efficiency drops. Your hands and feet lose dexterity, which affects bike handling, gear shifting, and the fine motor skills of T1 and T2.

Cold also suppresses your thirst mechanism. Athletes in cold conditions drink 30-40% less than in warm conditions at the same intensity, which leads to dehydration that goes unnoticed until the run.

### Specific Cold Adjustments

**Swim:** A full-sleeve wetsuit (if legal) provides insulation. In water below 16 degrees, neoprene swim caps, booties, and gloves are worth considering if race rules allow. The first 200-400 meters will feel shocking — focus on controlled breathing and don't sprint. Cold water triggers a gasp reflex that elevates heart rate independent of effort.

**Bike:** Dress in layers you can shed. A wind vest or light jacket over your tri suit adds meaningful warmth at minimal weight. Arm warmers and knee warmers are easy to remove and stuff in a jersey pocket if temperatures rise. Full-finger gloves preserve hand dexterity for braking and shifting.

On the pacing side, cold conditions don't require power reductions — in fact, cool temperatures (10-18 degrees) are physiologically optimal for endurance performance. However, if you're shivering and uncomfortable, your RPE will be elevated. Focus on maintaining your target watts rather than target heart rate, since heart rate may run lower in cool conditions.

**Nutrition:** Set a timer and eat on schedule. Without the thirst cue from sweating, athletes in cold conditions often arrive at T2 under-fueled. Aim for the same 60-80g of carbohydrates per hour you would target in warm conditions. Warm fluids from your bottle (thermos-style insulated bottles work well) are easier on the stomach in cold weather.

**Run:** Cold-weather runs actually favor performance once you're warmed up. The main risk is starting too fast because you feel fresh. Stick to your planned pace.

## Wind: Where Smart Athletes Gain the Most Time

Wind doesn't slow everyone equally. Athletes who understand aerodynamic drag gain 5-15 minutes over those who fight the wind at full power.

### The Physics of Wind

Aerodynamic drag scales with the cube of your speed relative to the wind. This means a 20km/h headwind costs you far more energy than a 20km/h tailwind gives back. The net effect of equal headwind and tailwind over an out-and-back course is always slower than a calm day at the same power output.

### Specific Wind Adjustments

**Headwind segments:** Drop power by 5-10% below your flat-road target. This feels counterintuitive — you're going slower, so shouldn't you push harder? No. At lower speeds, each additional watt produces a smaller speed increase because drag is proportional to the cube of velocity. The energy cost of fighting wind at full power is disproportionate to the time gained.

**Tailwind segments:** Do not increase power. Accept the free speed and maintain your target watts. Many athletes unconsciously push harder with a tailwind because the speed feels easy. This wastes energy that would serve you better on the run.

**Crosswind:** Stability is the priority, especially for athletes on deep-section wheels. Grip your handlebars firmly, widen your elbows slightly for stability, and be prepared for gusts at gaps in hedgerows, buildings, or at road junctions. If crosswinds are strong (above 30km/h), consider swapping to a shallower front wheel if your race venue allows morning-of equipment changes. A 50mm front wheel in a strong crosswind can make you slower than a 30mm wheel because the time you lose fighting for control exceeds the aerodynamic advantage.

**Overall:** On a windy course, shift your pacing focus from power to RPE. Target the same perceived effort you'd hold on a calm day. Your speed will be lower on headwind sections and higher on tailwind sections, but your total energy expenditure stays controlled.

## Rain: Execution Changes, Not Pacing Changes

Rain doesn't meaningfully affect your metabolic demands. You don't need to reduce power or pace because it's raining. What changes is how you execute.

### Bike Adjustments

**Cornering:** Wet roads reduce tire grip by 20-30%. Take corners 3-5km/h slower than you would on dry roads. Keep the bike upright through turns rather than leaning — steer with your body weight, not the handlebar. Painted road markings, manhole covers, and metal grates become dangerously slick when wet. Ride around them.

**Braking:** Start braking 30-50% earlier than on dry roads. Rim brakes lose significant stopping power when wet (disc brakes are less affected). Feather your brakes lightly on the approach to corners to clear water from the braking surface before you need full stopping power.

**Vision:** Apply anti-fog spray to your glasses before the race. If rain is heavy, clear lenses or no glasses may be better than tinted lenses. A cycling cap with a brim under your helmet diverts water from your eyes.

**Tires:** If you know the course will be wet, consider running 5-10 PSI less than your normal dry pressure. The slightly larger contact patch improves grip with a minimal speed penalty.

### Run Adjustments

**Footwear:** Apply anti-blister lubricant (Body Glide or similar) to your feet in T2. Wet socks dramatically increase friction and blister risk. Some athletes pre-apply a thin layer of petroleum jelly to the insole of their running shoes.

**Footing:** Shorten your stride on wet surfaces, especially on painted markings, bridge decking, and aid station areas where spilled sports drink creates a slick surface. The small pace cost of shorter strides is negligible compared to the risk of a slip and fall.

## Combining Conditions: The Real-World Matrix

Race day rarely delivers a single weather variable. You might face heat and wind, or cold and rain, or altitude and heat. When conditions stack, the adjustments compound.

A useful rule of thumb: when two adverse conditions are present simultaneously, apply the larger single adjustment and add half of the smaller one. For example, if heat calls for a 5% power reduction on the bike and headwind calls for an 8% reduction on a specific segment, target approximately 10-11% below your fair-weather target on that segment (8% plus half of 5%).

The most dangerous combination is heat and humidity, because your primary cooling mechanism — sweat evaporation — is impaired. A 30-degree day at 40% humidity is significantly easier to manage than a 28-degree day at 85% humidity. Dew point is a better predictor of heat stress than air temperature alone. Above a dew point of 18 degrees Celsius (65 degrees Fahrenheit), add 20-30 seconds per mile to your run target beyond whatever temperature-based adjustments you've already made.
